01 — Introduction

The Question

How has the lyrical portrayal of femininity in K-pop girl group songs shifted from the second generation to the fourth? As the industry expanded from the Korean market to a global phenomenon, did the voices in these songs change — and if so, in what direction?

Between 2NE1 declaring "I am the best" in 2011 and NewJeans whispering "I'm super shy" in 2023, something shifted. But characterizing that shift is more complicated than it first appears. This project uses distant reading, a quantitative scoring method applied to the lyrical content, and combined with close reading of four representative songs to trace what changed and explore the forces behind those changes.

I have analyzed lyrics across four dimensions: how much agency the singers claim, the emotional tone of the song, how heavily physical appearance is referenced, and the overall concept style assigned by the label. Together these produce a composite "lyrical autonomy" score for each song on a 1–5 scale.

Rather than assuming a single narrative of progress or regression, this project asks an open question: what patterns emerge when trying to measure these dimensions across generations, and what might those patterns tell us about the relationship between market forces, globalization, and the construction of femininity in pop music?

02 — Corpus

The Dataset

45 title tracks from 15 girl groups, selected by chart performance (Melon Top 100 for 2009–2015, Spotify Korea Top 50 for 2016–2024) and balanced across three generational eras. Lyrics were collected via the Genius API + Python and scored through bilingual close reading.

03 — Methodology

How to Score

Each song was scored on four dimensions using a 1–5 Likert scale. Scores were assigned through bilingual close reading (Korean + English translations), cross-referenced with promotional context like news or comments.

LACA: Lyric Agency Content Analysis — Traditional NLP tools like VADER are English-only and measure a single axis (positive/negative sentiment). The corpus I collected has an average of 39.8% Korean characters which VADER would discard them all. Instead, I used LACA, a four-dimensional scoring rubric applied by a bilingual LLM (Claude, Anthropic) that reads Korean and English natively.

Each song is scored on four independent dimensions (1–5 scale), producing a composite index for generation-level comparison. Rationales serve as a qualitative audit trail.

Lyric Agency

Who acts? Who initiates?
1 — Passive, waiting, pleading5 — Self-determined, commanding

Emotional Tone

Emotional register and energy
1 — Vulnerable, melancholic5 — Fierce, empowered

Appearance Focus

Degree of body/beauty emphasis
1 — No body/beauty references5 — Heavy appearance focus

Concept Style

Label-assigned marketing framing
1 — Cute/submissive concept5 — Girl crush/power concept

Reading the Scales: For Agency, Tone, and Concept Style: 1 = fully passive, 5 = fully autonomous.

For Appearance Focus, the scale works inversely: 1 = no appearance emphasis, 5 = heavy appearance emphasis. A higher Appearance score does NOT indicate more autonomy.

The composite score averages Agency, Tone, and Concept Style (excluding Appearance, which is scored inversely). A composite of 5.0 represents maximum lyrical autonomy; 1.0 represents maximum passivity. Appearance Focus is reported separately to avoid conflating different types of measurement.

05 — Timeline

Context and Convergence

Lyrics do not exist separately or randomly. To understand shifts in how K-pop girl groups present femininity, we need to place those shifts alongside two parallel trajectories: the Korean feminist movement and K-pop's globalization. This timeline maps key cultural moments against our data.

2nd Generation (2009–2014): Domestic Market, Emerging Feminism

Korean Feminist Context

The early 2010s saw growing online feminist discourse in South Korea, but mainstream feminist activism had not yet reached the explosive visibility it would gain later. The "Gangnam murder" (2016) and #MeToo (2018) were still years away. Girl group concepts were largely shaped by domestic audience expectations, with "cute" (aegyo) concepts dominating alongside early "girl crush" acts like 2NE1 and 4Minute.

K-pop Market

K-pop's primary market was still domestic and pan-Asian. The "Korean Wave" (Hallyu) was building through dramas and variety shows, but Western mainstream breakthrough had not yet occurred. PSY's "Gangnam Style" (2012) was treated as a novelty rather than a blueprint.

What the Data Shows

  • Average composite score: 3.24 (moderate, wide range)
  • Korean language ratio: ~46% (substantial bilingual mixing, but Korean-dominant)
  • The generation spans everything from Girls' Generation's "Gee" (composite 1.33) to 2NE1's "I Am the Best" (composite 5.0), reflecting a domestic market that supported both cute and fierce concepts.

3rd Generation (2015–2019): Global Expansion Meets #MeToo

Korean Feminist Context

This period saw an extraordinary surge in Korean feminist activism. The Gangnam Station murder (2016) catalyzed a national conversation about misogyny. The Megalian feminist online community emerged and provoked fierce cultural debate. In 2018, South Korea's #MeToo movement brought down powerful public figures. The "escape the corset" (탈코르셋) movement encouraged women to reject beauty standards. Feminist books like Kim Ji-young, Born 1982 became bestsellers.

K-pop Market

BTS's global breakthrough (2017–2018) proved K-pop could sustain Western audiences. BLACKPINK became the highest-charting female K-pop act internationally. Labels began consciously producing for global consumption. English-language content increased, and "girl crush" became the dominant concept — aligning with both feminist discourse and international audience expectations.

What the Data Shows

  • Average composite score: 3.22 (nearly identical to 2nd gen)
  • Korean language ratio: ~46% (unchanged from 2nd gen)
  • Appearance focus drops from 2.0 to 1.6 — possibly reflecting "escape the corset" influence
  • Girl crush acts (BLACKPINK, Mamamoo) coexist with cute concepts (TWICE "What is Love?" at 1.33) — but girl crush dominates chart performance

4th Generation (2020–2024): Global-First Strategy, Diverging Paths

Korean Feminist Context

Post-#MeToo Korea saw a complicated backlash. Anti-feminist sentiment grew, particularly among young Korean men. The 2022 presidential election became partly defined by gender politics. Female idols faced increasing scrutiny — some were criticized for perceived feminist signals (e.g., Irene from Red Velvet reading feminist literature, short hairstyles). The cultural environment for overt feminist expression within K-pop became more fraught, even as global audiences embraced it.

K-pop Market

Fourth-gen groups launched into an industry built for global audiences from day one. HYBE, JYP, and SM all pursued international markets aggressively. Some groups (NewJeans, LE SSERAFIM) debuted with majority-English songs. The market diversified: labels simultaneously offered "soft" personas (NewJeans) and "fierce" personas (aespa, ITZY) to different audience segments.

What the Data Shows

  • Average composite score: 3.76 (the highest of any generation)
  • But also the highest standard deviation: 1.35 (vs. 1.15 for 2nd gen, 1.03 for 3rd gen)
  • Korean language ratio drops to ~27% — the sharpest generational decline
  • The generation contains both the highest-scoring songs (aespa "Savage" at 5.0, LE SSERAFIM "FEARLESS" at 5.0) and among the lowest (NewJeans "Super Shy" at 1.67)
  • Both extremes are heavily English-dominant — language shift is independent of agency direction
The Convergence: Three trends come together in the fourth generation: a domestic feminist backlash that makes overt empowerment messaging riskier at home; a global market that rewards both "relatable vulnerability" and "aspirational power"; and an English-language shift that separates lyrics from their Korean cultural context. The result is not a single change, but a growing range of options. Companies place labels in different positions on this range to reach different groups of people.
06 — Close Reading

Four Songs, Four Stories

Numbers reveal trends; lyrics reveal meaning. I selected four songs — one paradigmatic track per archetype — for bilingual close reading.

2NE1 — "I Am the Best" (2011)

2NE1 · YG Entertainment · 2nd generation · Girl Crush archetype
Agency 5/5 Tone 5/5 Appear. 3/5 Style 5/5

The peak of second-generation girl crush. The speaker asserts total dominance — the repeated declaration "내가 제일 잘 나가" (I'm the best) positions the female subject as self-authorizing and unchallengeable. Agency is absolute: there is no love interest, no plea, no vulnerability. The lyrics function as a manifesto of self-sufficiency.

Notably, appearance references are present but wielded as power, not as passivity — fashion and beauty serve the speaker's dominance rather than an observer's gaze. This distinction is critical to understanding how "appearance focus" operates differently across eras.

BLACKPINK — "DDU-DU DDU-DU" (2018)

BLACKPINK · YG Entertainment · 3rd generation · Girl Crush archetype
Agency 5/5 Tone 5/5 Appear. 3/5 Style 5/5

BLACKPINK's global breakout carries the girl crush torch into the third generation, but the mechanism shifts. Where 2NE1's agency was culturally grounded (Korean slang, domestic references), "DDU-DU DDU-DU" weaponizes luxury branding — the speaker's power is coded through wealth, fashion, and global cool rather than personal assertion alone.

This "brand-powered agency" raises a key question: does the singer own her power, or does the corporation's image machine? The composite score matches 2NE1's peak, but the source of authority has migrated from self to spectacle.

NewJeans — "Super Shy" (2023)

NewJeans · ADOR / HYBE · 4th generation · Cute/Soft archetype
Agency 2/5 Tone 2/5 Appear. 1/5 Style 1/5

With a composite of 1.67, "Super Shy" sits at the low end of the autonomy spectrum. The lyrics is defined by hesitation — "super shy" is repeated as a defining characteristic, not a temporary state. The sonic palette (gentle, breathy, Y2K nostalgia) reinforces softness through aesthetics.

Crucially, this is also one of the most English-dominant songs in our corpus (only 7% Korean). The global audience shapes not just language but persona: vulnerability becomes a universal emotional register, potentially replacing culturally-specific forms of Korean femininity. Whether this represents authentic softness, deliberate aesthetic choice, or market-calculated positioning is one of the central tensions this project explores.

LE SSERAFIM — "FEARLESS" (2022)

LE SSERAFIM · Source Music / HYBE · 4th generation · Girl Crush archetype
Agency 5/5 Tone 5/5 Appear. 1/5 Style 5/5

Same parent label (HYBE), same generation, opposite persona. LE SSERAFIM's "FEARLESS" scores a perfect 5.0 composite — maximum agency, maximum empowerment — yet it coexists on the same chart with "Super Shy." This coexistence is perhaps the data's most telling finding: the fourth generation does not move in one direction but expands in both.

The speaker rejects social expectations explicitly ("I'm gonna be a star"), with zero appearance references. But note: this too is heavily English-dominant (only 14% Korean). Both the most passive and most assertive fourth-generation songs speak overwhelmingly in English, suggesting globalization shapes the form of expression regardless of its content.

07 — Findings

Findings...?

The data does not support a simple narrative of declining agency. Instead, three key findings emerge:

Finding 1: Diversification, Not Decline

The fourth generation's average composite score (3.76) is actually the highest of any generation, exceeding both the second (3.24) and third (3.22). However, its standard deviation is also the widest (1.35 vs. 1.03 for 3rd gen). The middle ground has thinned: fourth-generation groups cluster more heavily at either end of the autonomy spectrum. This suggests not a regression in agency but an expansion of the range of femininity that the market supports.

Finding 2: Language as Market Signal

Korean lyric ratio shifts from ~46% (2nd and 3rd gen) to ~27% (4th gen). The sharpest drops correlate with HYBE-label groups targeting global audiences. However, language shift and agency shift are not synonymous — both the most and least autonomous fourth-generation songs are English-dominant. English functions as a market-access strategy independent of lyrical content.

Finding 3: Appearance De-emphasis Across the Board

Appearance focus decreases across all three generations (avg 2.0 → 1.6 → 1.3). Even groups with low agency scores, like NewJeans, avoid body-focused lyrics. This may reflect broader cultural shifts (including "escape the corset" influence) — or it may mean that the site of appearance emphasis has migrated from lyrics to visual media (music videos, styling, social media), which lies outside the scope of text-only analysis.

The evolution of femininity in fourth-generation K-pop is better understood as a market-driven diversification than a unidirectional shift. Labels simultaneously offer "fierce" and "soft" personas to different audience segments within the same global marketplace. The more productive question may not be whether agency has risen or fallen, but rather: who decides which version of femininity gets performed, for which audience, and to what end?
08 — Limitations

What This Study Cannot Capture

Text vs. visual: Text analysis captures what is said, not what is shown. Music videos, choreography, and styling are powerful image-construction tools that lie outside our corpus. A song scoring low on appearance in lyrics may still foreground bodies visually. The migration of appearance emphasis from lyrics to visual media is itself a finding worth further study.

Title track bias: This corpus includes only top-charting title tracks — the songs labels select as the primary commercial product. But many girl groups explore significantly different themes in their B-sides, album tracks, and less-promoted singles. For example, later TWICE releases like "I Can't Stop Me" and "SET ME FREE" move toward themes of female self-reflection and growth. Red Velvet's "7월 7일 (One of These Nights)" offers a somber meditation often read as an allusion to the Sewol Ferry tragedy. (G)I-DLE's "LION" is an anthem of female coronation and self-empowerment. These tracks suggest that the "range" of a group's artistic expression extends well beyond what chart data captures — and that the songs the market rewards most heavily may not represent the full spectrum of what artists create. This limitation is also, in a sense, a finding: it raises the question of whether chart performance reflects audience preference for certain types of femininity over others.

Scoring subjectivity: Scoring subjectivity remains a limitation. While I mitigated this through bilingual reading and explicit rubrics, a single scorer inevitably introduces bias. Inter-rater reliability testing with multiple scorers would strengthen future iterations of this framework.

Corpus size: A 45-song corpus across 15 groups provides a meaningful snapshot, but cannot capture the full complexity of the K-pop landscape. Solo debuts, sub-unit releases, self-produced content (increasingly significant in the fourth generation), and groups from smaller labels are all absent from this analysis.

Future Directions

Extending this framework to include music video visual analysis (using frame-level image tagging), fan reception data (comment sentiment analysis), self-composed vs. label-assigned song comparison, and a parallel analysis of boy group lyrics would yield a more comprehensive picture of gender performance in K-pop's global expansion.
